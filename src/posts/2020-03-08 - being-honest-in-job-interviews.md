Is it better to be honest that you don't know the answers to some of the questions? Is it worth overselling yourself — "fake it till you make it"? These were the questions I asked my colleagues last Friday, and I'm sharing some of their answers.

Personally, the former has always been better than the latter, and I've been doing that for the 4 years I've been in the tech industry. But I have a friend who says bluffing works, and for some, it really is part of their strategy. To me, the only **pro** of the "fake it till you make it" strategy is getting the job — the consequences follow later. But if you're honest, there are far more pros than just landing the role.

Here are the **pros** my friends and I experienced with the honesty path:

**Peace of mind.** If you're honest, you don't worry about the consequences. You're not setting unreasonable expectations. If a company accepts you despite the gaps (but with a drive to learn), that says a lot about their culture — and that's exactly what I look for.

**Comfortable discussion.** Honesty drives longer conversations. It becomes a two-way interview. You get to ask about things you don't know, and companies are usually willing to talk about how they work.

**Proves you're good to work with.** People who know what they *don't* know, paired with a good attitude, are the kind of people teams want to work with.

**You still get the job.** Getting hired is just the start. The real work begins after — closing the gaps you were upfront about.

The **con** is obvious — you might not get the job. But that just means you weren't the candidate they needed right now. On the bright side, you didn't leave a bad impression.

What are your thoughts?
